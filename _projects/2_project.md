Towards the end of my PhD, I started asking myself what to do next. It took me a while to admit that rodents weren't quite the level of complexity I felt most comfortable with. After a year of beautiful (and genuinely off-beat) explorations — from hibernating squirrels to neuromodulation in *C. elegans* — I came across a [paper](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32139539/) linking differences in individual brain wiring to behavioral variability, and knew I'd found the system I wanted to work on.

So I moved to Paris, to the brain development lab of [Bassem Hassan](https://www.thebraindevlab.com/about), and started learning a whole new language: genes, transcription factors, neuroblasts, progenitors... Words I now use with the false confidence of someone who, not long ago, thought that "cell fate" was something you'd read in a horoscope. I like this way of looking at behavior, and I think we can learn a lot about why animals do what they do by asking how the brain is built in the first place {% cite chrzanowska2026building %}.

I now study fruit flies, and specifically, I focus on the dorsal cluster neurons (also known as LC14) and a simple visual walking behavior that gives me plenty of puzzles to work on. I'll keep you posted on what we find.
