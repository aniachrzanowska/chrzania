I spent my PhD in the lab of [Karl Farrow](https://farrowlab.sites.vib.be/en#/) in Leuven, Belgium. This was my first experience studying animal behavior and trying to understand why animals do what they do. I became particularly interested in so-called innate behaviors (although we could discuss what "innate" means).

What attracted me was the idea that some behaviors emerge without any need for learning. Animals seem to come equipped with neural circuits that can generate remarkably complex responses from day one. To me, this felt like a great entry point for understanding how the brain produces behavior.

My research focused on the superior colliculus, a midbrain structure that helps animals react rapidly to important events in their environment. I studied how mice respond to visual stimuli that reliably trigger defensive behaviors such as freezing or escaping. I got interested in how different types of neurons contribute to these reactions and how relatively small circuits can set the activity of larger brain-wide networks.
